A self-build housing project with a lightweight platform construction system.

Year  :  2023
Leading tutor  :  David Grandorge & Ted Swift
Institution  :  London Metropolitan University

The site for the self-build housing scheme is located at 337-357 Kingsland Road, in Hackney. The site features a triangular shape with one edge adjacent to the bustling Kingsland road and one running along Enfield Road. The short edge of the site is positioned against the sidewall of the neighbouring building – Metropolitan house, which was built in 1886 as a hospital, now is used as a hostel and business centre. On the opposite side of the site is located a newly finished housing tower designed by Henley Hale-Brown. The site faces a crescent line of terraced houses. The site is based in the proximity of green public spaces such as Stonebridge Gardens and De Beauvoir Square.

Students were asked to work in teams to define an urban condition and massing in order to split the site between each member. The proposal consists of two elongated rectangular buildings each placed along two main streets, in consequence separating public areas from the private communal garden inside. A shorter building located along Enfield Road allows sunlight from the south to reach the garden. The building contains 13 single storey apartments and 2 maisonettes as well as a nursery for local residents. Two buildings come together at the acute angle of the site to form the main entrance to the garden and public space in front. The project focuses on the characteristics of the linear block based along Kingsland road which provides a total of 32 apartments. The project intended to deliver a mix of affordable compact dwellings to satisfy different lifestyles of residents and at the same time ensure its self-build objective.

On the ground floor are located 4 two-bedroom apartments as well as a café, entrances to the private garden, entrances to the building cores, bike storages and bin storages. All of the apartments have an entrance from the garden and are lifted from the street level to provide maximum privacy for the rooms facing the street. Bathrooms have a roof light for ventilation purposes but it also allows a slight amount of daylight from the lightwell above. On the first and second floor are located 10 two storeys maisonettes. Each of them has two bedrooms on the lower floor - both facing either garden or street side - and a living room and kitchen on the upper floor – a long room with a dual aspect. Apartments interlock each other and wrap around a light well.

The same system is used on the next two floors yet it has a more traditional layout of rooms with living and kitchen on the lower floor and bedrooms above. On the fifth floor are located 8 one-bedroom flats. Bathrooms and kitchens on all floors have windows to the light well which support cross ventilation as well as provide dimmed natural light. The top floor of the building also implements a shared balcony space for the apartments facing the street and a communal roof terrace with a view of the garden. The proposed design delivers a range of different types of dwellings to meet resident’s various living needs. However compact floor plan is recompensated by numerous shared facilities inspired by the essence of a self-build community.

The difference in the two main elevations highlights the opposite character of the street side and garden side of the building. The elevation facing Kingsland Road is more visually and acoustically enclosed, with smaller windows and solid balcony walls. This decision is a consequence of the less private essence of the busy street. The see-through element on the balcony balustrade doesn’t block the view when a person is seated while still maintaining a level of separation. West elevation is looking into the private garden within the scheme which recognise the need for a more open type. A considerably larger amount of glazing provides a view as well as south-west daylight.

The building features a 6 storey timber structure with a concrete foundation. The tectonic strategy for the project was influenced by the regular grid of 3.6m of structural walls parallel to the short edge of the building. This quality allowed for an uncommon system for a mid-rise building which is a traditional timber framing. 150x50mm studs are used for balloon farming for exterior and load-bearing walls placed 400mm centres from each other. Wherever continuity of structural wall is broken, because of wall openings, a beam is being introduced to ensure sturdiness. Stud walls contain noggings for additional rigidity strength. Noggings placement at 1200mm and 2400mm height provide an opportunity for straightforward installation of standard sheathing boards. Structure benefits from the use of CLT cores which eliminate shear forces and maintains the use of lightweight material. Floor joists are placed in 400 mm centres as well and align with the stud arrangement.

Another significant element of the elevation design is the use of two distinct materials. Cloured cladding will become an outer skin of the building while the white render is applied on all surfaces distinct from the skin surface. Employment of while smooth material will help with light reflection in more shadowed spaces, such as light wells or balconies pushed into the structure. The intention for the facade was to identify the material and technique which supports quick and straightforward assembly. The material used for the external skin is a corrugated panel system made from semi-compressed fiber cement. Standard 3050mm panels with the required 200mm vertical overlap accurately cover the height of one floor. Furthermore, you are able to see the vertical divisions between the panel but horizontal overlaps are seamless so it doesn't change the rhythm of the windows on the elevation.

The self-build aspect of the project at this scale is often associated with the procurement process. However, the proposed design creates an opportunity for future residents to get involved in the construction. The project requires seven skilled sub-contractors who will be responsible for the work undertaken on the site and the supervision of self-builders. Self-builders are expected to obtain CSCS (Construction Skills Certification Scheme) card as proof of the completed training delivering the basic construction qualification. The use of lightweight structural elements and easy straightforward fixings ensures a significant part of the job can be done without extensive knowledge just with the directions from the contractor. Moreover, interior walls within the apartments can be assembled by self-builders as well as wall linings as long as there are control checks made by the contractor. The compact nature of the apartments encourages residents to follow the proposed design yet some elements can be adapted. The most efficient layout of each apartment has been suggested in the project but the structural grid allows for flexibility when it comes to the placement of the interior walls.
